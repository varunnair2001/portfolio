Snow Removal Accessibility

Project Overview

Snow removal is a routine but physically demanding task in snow-prone regions such as the Northeastern United States. For older adults, individuals with arthritis or back problems, and users with limited strength or mobility, clearing snow from a driveway can pose significant safety and accessibility challenges. While snowblowers reduce physical effort, they are often expensive, require maintenance, depend on fuel or batteries, and occupy substantial storage space. Manual shoveling, on the other hand, places high strain on the back, shoulders, and wrists.


This project sought to design a snow removal device that is low-cost, easy to use, compact, and safe, with a particular focus on users who struggle with existing solutions due to age, disability, or financial constraints. The outcome was a fulcrum-based, wheeled snow shovel that reduces lifting effort while maintaining the familiarity and reliability of a manual tool.


Problem Definition & Context

1. Problem Statement -

It is difficult for some people to clear snow from their driveways due to age-related limitations, disabilities, or musculoskeletal conditions. Snowblowers can make clearing snow easier but are often prohibitively expensive, require storage space many users do not have, and introduce maintenance and reliability issues. There is a need for a snow removal device that:


Makes Snow Moving Easier

Is Cheap to Purchase & Maintain

Takes Up Minimal Storage Space


2. Social, Ethical & Environmental Context -

Snow removal is not only a convenience issue but also an equity and accessibility concern. In regions with frequent snowfall, individuals who cannot safely remove snow may lose access to their homes, transportation, or essential services. Seniors who wish to age in place are particularly affected. From an ethical standpoint, the design needed to:


Be Safe for Vulnerable Populations

Avoid introducing unnecessary environmental impact (e.g., fuel-powered systems)

Support independence without requiring advanced technical knowledge


Users & Stakeholders

Target Users -

  • 1. Older adults (40–80+) experiencing reduced strength or mobility

  • 2. Individuals with arthritis, back problems, or other physical limitations

  • 3. Users who cannot afford or store a snowblower


Secondary Stakeholders -

  • 1. Caregivers and family members who assist with snow removal

  • 2. Community organizations advocating for accessibility

  • 3. Manufacturers and retailers of affordable consumer tools


Design Process & Thinking

The project began with broad brainstorming around snow removal solutions. While these concepts explored automation and novelty, many were ruled out early due to cost, complexity, energy dependence, storage constraints, or feasibility concerns. Initial ideas included:


  • 1. Robotic or remote-controlled plows ("Roomba plow")

  • 2. Battery-powered compact snow sweepers

  • 3. Heated driveway mats or blankets

  • 4. Modular or improved electric snow shovels

  • 5. Vacuum-based or melting systems


Structured Design Thinking -

The team used several structured design thinking methods. Through these exercises, the team identified that reducing lifting effort, rather than eliminating manual control entirely, was the most impactful and feasible direction:


  • 1. Objective Trees: To prioritize ease of use, affordability, compactness, durability, and safety

  • 2. Mind Mapping: To break down snow removal into user effort, environmental conditions, storage, cost, and maintenance

  • 3. SCAMPER Method: To systematically rethink the traditional snow shovel

Human Factors & Ergonomic Considerations

Human factors principles were central throughout the project:

  • 1. Snow shoveling is known to place high loads on the lower back and shoulders

  • 2. Lifting motions are particularly risky for older adults

  • 3. Users value tools that feel familiar and predictable, especially when safety is a concern


Key ergonomic goals include:

  • 1. Reducing spinal loading by minimizing lift

  • 2. Encouraging pushing motions rather than pulling or lifting

  • 3. Improving grip comfort and wrist posture

  • 4. Maintaining balance and stability during use


These considerations directly informed the mechanical architecture of the final design.

User Research

The research aimed to understand:

  • 1. Current snow removal practices

  • 2. Physical challenges & limitations

  • 3. Cost sensitivity & budget constraints

  • 4. Storage availability

  • 5. Preferences for manual vs. automated tools


A survey-based study was selected to reach a broad and diverse sample efficiently. Participants were recruited from team members’ hometowns across the Northeastern U.S., including friends, parents, and grandparents. The survey included:


  • 1. Demographic questions (age, location, health considerations)

  • 2. Questions about current snow removal methods

  • 3. Ranking tasks to assess priorities (cost, effort, storage, time)

  • 4. Open-ended questions on desired improvements


Key Findings

  • 1. 61% of respondents remove snow themselves

  • 2. 35% reported preexisting conditions (e.g., arthritis, back problems)

  • 3. Ease of use and labor reduction were consistently ranked as top priorities

  • 4. Only 12% regularly used snowblowers; many relied on shovels


Users expressed concerns about Maintenance, Storage space, and Trust in automated systems. An unexpected insight was the importance of hand warmth and grip comfort, with respondents mentioning waterproof gloves and heated or insulated handles.


Impact on Design Direction -

Initially, the team prioritized automation. User feedback challenged this assumption, revealing skepticism toward robotic solutions and a preference for simple, reliable, low-maintenance tools. This insight drove the shift toward a manual, ergonomically enhanced design.

Design Evolution

Based on research and feasibility analysis, the team narrowed to a redesigned manual shovel incorporating:

A Fulcrum System

Wheels for Rolling rather than Carrying Snow

Ergonomic Handles


This approach preserved the familiarity of a shovel while fundamentally changing the physical demands of use.

Final Design Description

The final design is a fulcrum-based snow shovel with wheels, intended to reduce physical strain while remaining affordable and compact.


Key Components -

  • 1. Shovel Blade: Wide aluminum blade for efficient snow movement

  • 2. Fulcrum System: Pivoting mechanism that converts lifting into a downward push

  • 3. Ergonomic Handles: Angled grips to reduce wrist and back strain

  • 4. Wheels: Polyurethane wheels that allow snow to be rolled rather than lifted


How the Design Addresses the User Needs -

  • 1. Ease of Use: Downward pushing motion is easier than lifting

  • 2. Affordability: Estimated total cost under $100

  • 3. Safety: Reduced back strain lowers injury risk

  • 4. Portability & Storage: Compact, modular design fits in closets or small garages

  • 5. Reliability: Manual operation eliminates charging or fuel concerns


Prototyping & Evaluation

Evaluation Methods -

  • 1. Weight Simulation Test: A 9 lb weight was used to simulate lifting snow

  • 2. Snow Pickup Simulation: Paper objects were used to test snow movement mechanics

  • 3. Cost and Size Evaluation: Compared against initial design constraints


Findings -

  • 1. Users reported minimal back strain during lifting tests

  • 2. The majority of effort was transferred to the arms rather than the lower back

  • 3. The device successfully moved simulated snow

  • 4. Cost and storage goals were met


One team member noted: “I didn’t feel anything at all—there was no stress on my back.”


Limitations & Trade-Offs

  • 1. Testing was conducted in simulated conditions rather than real snow

  • 2. The wheels added weight, making stair transport more difficult

  • 3. Feedback from a broader target population would strengthen validation


Despite these limitations, the prototype demonstrated proof of concept and strong alignment with user needs.

Innovation & Impact

The innovation in this project lies not in automation, but in mechanical reframing informed by human factors. By applying ergonomic principles to a familiar tool, the design achieves meaningful reductions in physical strain without increasing complexity or cost. This solution supports:


  • 1. Aging in place

  • 2. Increased independence for users with physical limitations

  • 3. More equitable access to safe snow removal tools

Reflection & Future Work

This project underscored the importance of grounding design decisions in real user data rather than assumed preferences. User research directly reshaped the team’s understanding of what innovation meant in this context. Future work would include:


  • 1. Real-world testing in varied snow conditions

  • 2. Wheel redesign to reduce weight

  • 3. Expanded testing with elderly and mobility-limited users


Overall, the project demonstrates how human-centered design and human factors engineering can produce practical, inclusive solutions to everyday problems.
